# Selected Excerpts for First-Pass Agent Evaluation

These excerpts are included so an agent can test the Creeklight vocabulary without ingesting the full 103k-word manuscript. They are not a substitute for the full source.

## 1. Thresholds / hidden-map logic

> “Compression points,” said Capybara at almost the same time, eyes moving toward the open place.
>
> “Sightline conversions,” said Red panda.
>
> They all looked at her.
>
> She flicked one ear. “Places where something above can stop merely observing and begin predicting. Branch fork to crossing. Crossing to bank edge. A route is different once it commits.”
>
> Axolotl, still gazing at the bark strip, said, “Places where the world expects belief.”
>
> No one answered that immediately.
>
> Turtle did.
>
> “Thresholds,” he said.
>
> The word settled over the gathering with a kind of old rightness.
>
> Otter stared down at the bark strip and felt his skin go cold in patches.
>
> The problem was larger than sabotage. Larger than route damage. Larger, even, than a set of enemies with well-matched specialties.
>
> Someone had learned where the creek changed state.
>
> Where water became footing.
>
> Where footing became crowd.
>
> Where crowd became panic.
>
> Where beauty became invitation.
>
> Where reflection became instruction.
>
> Where a known path became trust, and trust became something you could lean your weight on without thinking about it.
>
> And then that someone had started pressing there.

## 2. Living duty versus sterile order

> That was when Pangolin spoke.
>
> Until that moment he had said almost nothing, and Otter understood suddenly that his silence had not been uncertainty. It had been pressure finding its moral footing.
>
> “No,” Pangolin said.
>
> The word was quiet.
>
> It changed the whole scene.
>
> Scarab turned toward him.
>
> Pangolin stepped forward one measured pace, claws sinking slightly into the mud at the shelf’s edge. He was not large in the way Wombat was large, nor atmospherically commanding in the way Capybara was. But when Pangolin occupied a line, it became hard to imagine any honest thing passing through it carelessly.
>
> “Duty,” Pangolin said, “is what you accept for the sake of the living. Not what you impose on them to make them legible.”
>
> The creek seemed, for one second, to draw itself inward around the sentence.
>
> Scarab’s answer came without visible irritation, which made it more chilling rather than less.
>
> “Living things misname preference as goodness all the time.”
>
> Pangolin did not yield a grain. “And ordered things misname obedience as safety.”

## 3. Care creates structure, but not sterile structure

> “Wombat anchors,” said Capybara.
>
> Wombat grunted once.
>
> “Pangolin calls the line,” said Otter.
>
> Pangolin looked at him then, surprised perhaps, but not opposed.
>
> Capybara said nothing. Which in that moment felt very close to approval.
>
> The creek darkened by another shade while they began, not removing the shells yet, but assembling the answer to their removal.
>
> Not cleanly.
>
> Not elegantly.
>
> Livingly.
>
> And as Otter bent to take his place in that larger pattern, he understood the next thing Platypus had gotten right from the wrong direction:
>
> care did create structure.
>
> It was just the kind you had to choose again every time.

## 4. Joy is necessary, not omnipotent

> Quokka moved into the open place and resumed doing small useful things because that was still what evening required.
>
> She handed a child a cup.
>
> Adjusted a mat.
>
> Redirected a conversation away from an edge it had not needed.
>
> But the motion was different now.
>
> Not false.
>
> Just stripped.
>
> Joy was still labor.
>
> It was still necessary.
>
> It was not omnipotent.

## 5. Care as restraint rather than immediate effect

> Chinchilla did not mistake it for success.
>
> She understood, finally, that this would not be solved in a scene.
>
> Her tenderness could not catch Hummingbird out of the air.
>
> Could not outrun terror.
>
> Could not persuade a broken vigilance back into stillness on request.
>
> All it could do, for now, was stop making the wound prove itself harder.
>
> The realization hurt in a different place than Quokka’s had hurt the night before, but it belonged to the same family of truths.
>
> Care was real.
>
> Care was not omnipotent.

## 6. Later recurrence of threshold logic

> Tree frog rubbed one thumb over bark. “Reply. Handoff. Water pressure.”
>
> Turtle, who had apparently already been near the crossing in the way one came to expect from him and still never liked being reminded of, said, “Thresholds again.”
>
> That tightened the air by one exact degree.
>
> Otter felt the old heat in his body at once.
> Not panic.
> Preparation with a bruise in it.
>
> Platypus had been pressing thresholds since Chapter 13. That was the whole hidden-map logic: not attacking only places, but the moments when one state became another—water to bank, route to reply, gathering to crowd, beauty to invitation, care to repair. The creek had answered by becoming harder to read, less central, more distributed, more lived in the expensive way Red panda had named. That answer had held. But it had not yet disproven Platypus.
> It had only made him work harder.
